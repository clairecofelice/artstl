Apotheosis of
St. Louis

Stop 2 of 6

The next stop on our tour is
the Apotheosis of St. Louis, one of the city's most recognizable symbols.

00

Quick Facts

27 feet tall

Designed by Charles Niehaus

Unveiled in 1906

Made from bronze and granite

01

Once at Art Hill,

look up at the museum.

From its iconic ledge in front of the museum, the Apotheosis of St. Louis

appears ready to leap into the air. However, the statue was not always in this location. The original plaster model of the statue of the Crusader King, Louis IX of France, stood at the main entrance to the 1904 World’s Fair (where the Missouri History Museum is now located). After the Fair concluded, the Louisiana Purchase Exposition Company donated a bronze version of the statue to Forest Park as a permanent reminder of the event.

02

When you first see the

statue how do you feel?

The equestrian statue captures Louis IX as a soldier in a manner intended to inspire

awe and respect. His horse appears to move forward while the draperies seem
to flutter and his magnificent ermine cape decorated with the symbol of French monarchy, the fleur-de-lis, indicates royalty. The raised sword, held hilt upwards to form a cross, implies his leadership in battle.

03

Now, look at the pedestal

and read the inscriptions.

On the north side of the pedestal are the words, "Presented to the City of St. Louis

by the Louisiana Purchase Exposition in commemoration of the Universal Exposition of 1904 held on this site." Also inscribed on the pedestal is the line "Designed by C. H. Niehaus," a reference
to the original artist, Charles Niehaus, who offered to create a bronze version of the plaster model for a $90,000 commission. However, the company took a lower $37,500 bid from a local artist, W. R. Hodges. In response, Niehaus sued the company for infringement of his intellectual property rights and was awarded $3,000.

04

Do you notice anything

strange about the sword?

It turns out, the sword St. Louis holds today is not the original. Over the years,

his sword has been broken or stolen a number of times. It was replaced in 1970, 1972, 1977 and 1981. Stealing, and later returning, the sword was considered a rite of passage for students in the engineering program at nearby Washington University.
